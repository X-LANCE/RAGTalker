Gate Score Map Visualization

Motion-Guided 2D Texture Fusion is the core innovation of our paper, with Gate Score serving as a crucial subcomponent.

To validate its effectiveness, we visualize how changing motion signals drive a single reference image to reconstruct the entire video, while additional key frames are fed into the fusion module for enhanced motion cues.

We provide heatmaps to illustrate the gating process, where regions closer to red denote higher gating scores—indicating increased reliance on information from the corresponding reference frame. By dynamically adjusting each reference texture's contribution, the Gate Score ensures that key frames with more relevant motion cues are weighted more heavily in the fusion process.

This is just an example of using multi-view images to enhance the multi-view capabilities of video generation. In practice, it can also be applied to scenarios such as background filling and texture supplementation for unseen poses.

The video at the bottom does not include additional reference-frame fusion. Since multi-view data is difficult to obtain and our dataset was not trained using multi-view data, you’ll notice that reconstruction fails in some cases with extreme viewing angles.

Audio-Driven / Video Self-Reenactment

This section aims to evaluate both audio-driven talking head generation and face reenactment, defined as follows:

  • Face Reenactment: To assess rendering performance, we use only the first frame of a video and subsequent motion-driven signals to reconstruct the original video sequence. By comparing the synthesized results to the ground-truth footage, we can effectively measure the accuracy and fidelity of the rendering process.
  • Audio-Driven Talking Head Generation: To examine editing capability, we start from a static initial frame and drive it with an audio track to create a new video. This setup reflects practical scenarios that require substantial or even complete editing, with full-sequence generation serving as a rigorous test of content-based editing.
In these tasks, key frames serve as additional reference material that the algorithm can access for extra texture information. Unless otherwise specified, all examples in this section use three reference frames and apply k-means clustering to guide the selection of these key frames.

Video Editing Case

This section evaluates the algorithm's text-based editing potential capabilities, specifically in five key operations:

  • Substituting: Replaces certain words or segments
  • Adding: Inserts extra text
  • Deleting: Removes specified content
  • Translating: Performs cross-lingual conversions
  • Continuing content: Generates new material appended to existing text

We employ our DiT module, which masks out the regions to be edited, then guides the model to incorporate both contextual cues and new audio signals.

When the updated audio content does not align with the existing video, the algorithm removes or inserts frames accordingly. For continuing content, we flip the video to maintain consistent frame counts before mask-filling. All audio in this section is generated using edge tts.

Ablation Study

This section presents our ablation study, designed to evaluate both the key modules in our framework and two reference frame selection strategies—Random Frame (using random frame counts) versus Clustered Frame (Ours, using a clustering approach). From left to right, the columns depict the Source Image (providing texture), GT (providing motion), W/O M.D., AVG, W/O F.M., Hard Fusion, Random Frame, and Clustered Frame (Ours).

The abbreviations above represent our key components: motion difference (M.D.), feature matching (F.M.), and soft gating. Each ablation experiment systematically evaluates their individual contributions to the overall synthesis quality. Notably, all experiments except for "Random Frame" utilize our key frame selection strategy, which demonstrably improves background completion quality. When any module is removed, visible flickering appears in the rendered output, indicating the model's reduced capability to effectively perform texture fusion across frames.

Ethical Consideration

The potential misuse of lifelike digital human face generation, such as for creating fraudulent identities or disseminating misinformation, necessitates preemptive ethical measures. Before utilizing these models, it is crucial for organizations to integrate ethical guidelines into their policies, ensuring the application of this technology emphasizes consent, transparency, and accountability. Furthermore, it is recommended to embed visible or invisible digital watermarks in any generated content.
